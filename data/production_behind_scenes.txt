Production and Behind the Scenes

Creators: Michael DiMartino and Bryan Konietzko

Avatar: The Last Airbender was created by Michael DiMartino and Bryan Konietzko, often referred to together as "Bryke" by fans. The two met while working at Nickelodeon Animation Studios and developed the concept for Avatar together. DiMartino had been watching a documentary about people trapped at the South Pole and was sketching a character of a powerless old man, while Konietzko turned it into a young boy with an arrow on his head. The two merged their ideas into what would become Aang and the world of Avatar.

The show premiered on Nickelodeon on February 21, 2005 and ran for three seasons (Books), concluding with the four-part finale Sozin's Comet on July 19, 2008. It was an immediate critical and commercial success and is widely considered one of the greatest animated television series ever made.

DiMartino and Konietzko went on to create The Legend of Korra, which premiered on Nickelodeon in 2012 and ran for four seasons, concluding in 2014. The show was moved from Nickelodeon's television schedule to an online-only release midway through its run, which affected its ratings but not its critical reputation.

Art Style and Influences

Avatar: The Last Airbender drew heavily from Asian cultures, mythologies, and art styles. The world-building was inspired primarily by Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Tibetan, and Inuit cultures. Each of the four nations' aesthetics, architecture, clothing, and customs were based on real-world Asian cultures — the Water Tribes drew from Inuit culture, the Earth Kingdom from Chinese culture, the Fire Nation from Japanese culture, and the Air Nomads from Tibetan Buddhist monk culture.

The four bending styles were each based on a real martial arts discipline with a corresponding animal teacher. Airbending was based on Baguazhang (learned from the flying bison), waterbending on Tai Chi (learned from the moon and ocean), earthbending on Hung Ga kung fu (learned from badgermoles), and firebending on Northern Shaolin kung fu (learned from the dragons). Martial arts consultant Sifu Kisu worked with the show's animators to ensure each bending style was accurate and distinct.

Voice Cast

The original voice cast of Avatar: The Last Airbender included Zach Tyler Eisen as Aang, Mae Whitman as Katara, Jack DeSena as Sokka, Jessie Flower as Toph, Dante Basco as Zuko, Grey DeLisle as Azula, and Mako Iwamatsu as Iroh in Season 1 and part of Season 2. After Mako's death from esophageal cancer in 2006, the role of Iroh was taken over by Greg Baldwin, who worked with Mako's family to honor his performance. The Tales of Ba Sing Se episode was dedicated to Mako's memory.

The Legend of Korra featured Janet Varney as Korra, David Faustino as Mako, P.J. Byrne as Bolin, Seychelle Gabriel as Asami Sato, and J.K. Simmons as Tenzin. Dante Basco reprised his role as Zuko in his elder years.

Music

The music of Avatar: The Last Airbender and The Legend of Korra was composed by the Track Team, consisting of Jeremy Zuckerman and Benjamin Wynn. The score blended Eastern and Western musical traditions to create a unique sound that matched the show's multicultural world. The iconic theme of the original series uses traditional Chinese instruments alongside orchestral arrangements. The Legend of Korra's music incorporated jazz influences to reflect the show's 1920s-inspired Republic City setting. Jeremy Zuckerman has since become one of the most acclaimed composers in animation, and the Avatar soundtrack is frequently cited as among the best in television animation history.

The Live Action Film

In 2010, director M. Night Shyamalan released a live action film adaptation of the first season of Avatar: The Last Airbender titled The Last Airbender. The film was widely considered a critical and creative failure, receiving significant backlash from fans for its casting choices, mispronunciation of character names (Aang was pronounced "Ung," Sokka as "Soh-ka," and Avatar as "Ahvatar"), and failure to capture the spirit of the original series. DiMartino and Konietzko were not involved in the film's creative decisions.

Netflix Adaptation

Netflix announced a live action adaptation of Avatar: The Last Airbender which began production with DiMartino and Konietzko attached as showrunners. However, both creators departed the project in 2020, citing creative differences with Netflix over their vision for the show. The Netflix live action series continued production without them and was released in 2024. The series received mixed reviews from fans and critics, with some appreciating the production values and others feeling it failed to capture the depth of the original.

Legacy

Avatar: The Last Airbender is consistently ranked among the greatest animated series ever made and has had a lasting cultural impact. It was praised for its complex characters, mature storytelling, diverse representation, themes of redemption and pacifism, and willingness to explore difficult subjects including war, genocide, trauma, and moral ambiguity in a children's show. It was re-released on Netflix in 2020 and immediately became one of the platform's most watched shows, introducing an entirely new generation of fans to the series over a decade after its original run.
